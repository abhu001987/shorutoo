From Survival to Scale: Unlocking India's MSME Potential

How algorithmic lending, digital common facilities, and e-commerce export hubs can transform 7.47 crore enterprises into global value chain powerhouses

By Shoruto economic affairs team

India's MSME sector acts as a vital macroeconomic driver, contributing 31.1% to GDP and employing over 32.8 crore individuals. However, its transition from survival to scale is severely impeded by a ₹30 lakh crore credit gap, acute payment delays, and a structural "dwarfishness" trap where 99% remain micro-enterprises. Unlocking their global competitiveness requires pivoting to cash-flow-based algorithmic lending, establishing plug-and-play digital common facilities, and institutionalizing e-commerce export hubs.

What are Micro, Small, and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs)?

About: Defining the Backbone of the Economy

MSMEs are the foundational pillars of an economy, encompassing businesses engaged in the manufacturing, production, processing, or preservation of goods, as well as the delivery of services. Often referred to as the "backbone of the economy," they drive industrial development, foster grassroots entrepreneurship, and create massive employment opportunities at lower capital costs than large-scale corporations.

Official Classification Framework

The government defines MSMEs based on a composite, dual-criterion framework that combines Investment in Plant & Machinery/Equipment and Annual Turnover. To remain in a specific category, an enterprise must satisfy both limits; if it crosses either threshold, it is automatically pushed into the higher classification tier.

How do MSMEs Drive India's Economic Growth and Development?

Macroeconomic Value Addition and GDP Acceleration

The MSME sector serves as a primary driver of domestic production and industrial capital formation across both manufacturing and service value chains. Rather than acting solely as final product manufacturers, small-scale enterprises specialize in producing key intermediate goods and precision assemblies that directly builds domestic industrial capacity, supporting the broader Atmanirbhar Bharat vision. According to the Economic Survey 2025-26, MSMEs account for approximately 31.1% of India's Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and command a substantial 35.4% share of manufacturing output.

Job-Rich Industrialization and Demographic Absorption

As a highly labor-intensive sector, MSMEs act as an essential economic stabilizer by absorbing surplus rural and semi-urban labor at lower capital-to-labor ratios than heavy, automated industries. The sector stands as the 2nd-largest employer in India after agriculture, providing livelihoods to over 32.8 crore individuals across 7.47 crore enterprises (Economic Survey 2025-26). Labor-intensive clusters like the textile and apparel hubs in Tirupur (Tamil Nadu) and the leather goods clusters in Kanpur (Uttar Pradesh) absorb millions of semi-skilled workers, converting India's demographic dividend into direct manufacturing productivity.

Powering Global Trade and Merchandise Exports

MSMEs are central to India's foreign exchange earnings, driving global trade integration across both traditional handicrafts and modern technical components. Official data highlights that MSMEs contribute 48.58% of India's total exports. To further boost this position, the government launched the dedicated Export Promotion Mission (EPM) with an outlay of ₹25,060 crore spanning from FY26 to FY31. Specialized production ecosystems—such as the auto-component clusters in Pune, electronics manufacturing setups in Noida, and gems and jewelry workshops in Surat—supply high-quality components directly into Global Value Chains (GVCs), strengthening India's international trade balance.

Fostering Inclusive Grassroots and Regional Growth

Unlike large-scale industries that tend to cluster around prime tier-1 urban zones, MSMEs naturally scale across tier-2, tier-3, and rural districts, which helps prevent uneven regional development. The Ministry of MSME's Udyam Registration Portal highlights this balanced distribution: while industrial states like Maharashtra lead with over 1.01 crore registrations (~13%), states like Uttar Pradesh follow closely with 86.3 lakh units (~11%), showing significant growth in semi-urban business hubs. By using programs like the Prime Minister's Employment Generation Programme (PMEGP) and the Scheme of Fund for Regeneration of Traditional Industries (SFURTI), the government builds shared infrastructure and common facility centers in distant rural pockets, creating local non-farm income streams and slowing rural-to-urban migration.

Democratizing Entrepreneurship for Marginalized Groups

The low initial capital required to set up an MSME provides a viable pathway for financial independence among traditionally underserved communities. As of early 2026, women-led enterprises account for approximately 40% of all registered MSMEs, totaling over 3.11 crore registrations across the Udyam and Udyam Assist platforms. PMEGP supported over 3.2 lakh women-owned enterprises, with women making up 39% of all beneficiaries. Public procurement from SC/ST-owned Micro and Small Enterprises (MSEs) increased from about ₹99 crore in 2015-16 to over ₹3,731 crore in 2024-25.

Accelerating Formalization and Financial Inclusion

The historic transition of small businesses from the unorganized shadow economy into the formal banking system expands the national tax base and builds verifiable data footprints. Total formal registrations on the Udyam Portal have crossed 7.47 crore units backed by credit infrastructure like the Credit Guarantee Fund Trust for Micro and Small Enterprises (CGTMSE).

Upstream Integration with Large Industries and Digital Public Procurement

MSMEs function as critical supply chain anchors, providing specialized tooling, casting, and maintenance services to major automotive, defense, and infrastructure corporations. Public procurement rules legally mandate that Central Ministries, Departments, and Central Public Sector Enterprises (CPSEs) source at least 25% of their annual requirements directly from Micro and Small Enterprises (MSEs). By February 2025, Government e-Marketplace (GeM) registered over 22 lakh sellers, including 29,000+ startups and 1.8 lakh women-led businesses, creating a highly competitive procurement ecosystem.

Driving High-Tech Innovation and Risk Capital Injection

The modern MSME landscape has evolved past low-tech manufacturing to encompass precision defense components, green technology assemblies, and medical instrumentation. Supported by advanced MSME Tool Rooms and Technology Centres, specialized engineering small businesses now manufacture critical satellite sub-assemblies for ISRO and tier-2 and tier-3 structural aerospace components, boosting India's global innovation standing. By 2023, India's 18 technology centres trained 16 lakh youth. Proving this expertise, the Bhubaneswar Tool Room manufactured 54,000 aerospace components for the Chandrayaan-3 mission.

What are the Key Bottlenecks in the Indian MSME Sector?

Staggering Credit Gap and Collateral Dependencies

Despite extensive policy interventions, the massive mismatch between formal capital supply and the actual financing demands of smaller firms remains a major obstacle. Industry data from SIDBI highlights a severe credit deficit in the MSME ecosystem, with an estimated ₹30 lakh crore shortfall representing roughly 24% of the sector's aggregate credit demand. Traditional commercial banks continue to rely on rigid collateral-backed underwriting standards. This practice heavily disadvantages smaller entities. An ICRIER report shows unincorporated non-agricultural enterprises averaged just ₹3.18 lakh in fixed assets (dropping to ₹2.15 lakh in manufacturing) in 2022-23, making steep bank collateral requirements very difficult to meet.

Chronic Cash Flow Crises Driven by Delayed Payments

A persistent lack of payment discipline among large corporate buyers, public sector enterprises, and government departments continuously drains the working capital of smaller firms. Even though the MSME Development Act, 2006 legally mandates a 45-day payment window, enforcement remains low due to fear of losing business contracts. While the strict integration of Section 43B(h) of the Income Tax Act, 1961 was introduced to penalize late corporate payments, large buyers often respond by shifting their business away from registered MSMEs to avoid regulatory scrutiny, leaving small suppliers highly vulnerable.

Deep Asymmetry in Modern Technology Adoption

While digital payment acceptance has scaled rapidly at the retail level, deep integration of automated operations, industrial machinery, and advanced digital enterprise tools remains heavily lopsided. According to the 'Understanding the Indian MSME Sector: Progress, and Challenges' report released by SIDBI, shows that while 90% of Indian MSMEs accept digital payments, a mere 18% have accessed digital lending platforms. This creates a severe productivity gap, causing Indian MSMEs to operate at just 18% of the productivity of large enterprises, compared to the 45-70% productivity levels seen across OECD nations.

Severe Vulnerability to Raw Material Price Shocks

Lacking bulk bargaining power, small units absorb the full force of commodity price spikes. This pressure forces them to fulfill long-term fixed-price supply orders at a net loss, directly resulting in sudden spikes in micro-enterprise bankruptcies. In key labor-intensive manufacturing sectors like the textile clusters of Tirupur or the metallurgical engineering hubs in Coimbatore, sudden price spikes in raw cotton, steel sheets, and specialty chemicals regularly wipe out operating margins.

Weak Infrastructure and Fragmented Logistics Networks

Frequent power quality issues force smaller manufacturing units to rely heavily on expensive diesel generators, which drives up production costs. Additionally, the lack of affordable, shared local labs means small units must pay steep fees to distant tier-1 cities to get their export quality certifications verified. The brassware manufacturers in Moradabad or the leather clusters in Kanpur face significantly higher inland transport costs than international competitors.

"Dwarfishness" Trap and Scale Inhibitions

The vast majority of Indian MSMEs remain trapped in a state of microscopic scale, intentionally choosing not to grow larger to avoid losing small-business benefits. Of the more than 7.47 crore formal enterprises registered on the government's Udyam portal, micro-enterprises make up over 99% of the total volume, while true medium-sized enterprises represent a tiny fraction.

Structural Deficits in Industry-Ready Vocational Skilling

A severe skills mismatch between traditional educational training programs and the rapidly evolving technological needs of modern manufacturing lines limits industrial output. Finding specialized technical labor—such as precision CNC (Computer Numerical Control) machine operators, industrial automation technicians, and certified quality inspectors—remains a major challenge for expanding small businesses. Operating on razor-thin margins, small firms cannot afford extensive in-house training. This, combined with a lack of localized, industry-driven curriculum at nearby vocational training centers, leaves small factories stuck with outdated, low-productivity manual assembly methods.

What Strategies can be Implemented to Boost Indian MSMEs?

Cash-Flow Based Algorithmic Lending Sandbox

Establish a regulatory sandbox combining the Udyam registration portal, GSTN, and Account Aggregator (AA) network to mandate cash-flow-based underwriting. Instead of physical property, banks should dynamically assess real-time invoice matching, continuous tax compliance, and digital ledger health to disburse automated, short-ticket working capital loans within hours.

TReDS Securitisation and Secondary Market Deepening

Create a specialized sovereign-backed clearing house to pool verified TReDS receivables into Asset-Backed Securities (ABS). These ABS units can be institutionalized and traded on secondary markets. By allowing commercial banks to count investments in these micro-enterprise trade blocks toward their Priority Sector Lending (PSL) targets, private institutional capital can be seamlessly channeled into MSME liquidity.

Plug-and-Play Digital Thread Co-Working Hubs

Establish specialized "Digital Common Facilities" across existing tier-II and tier-III manufacturing hubs. These hubs should house shared advanced infrastructure—such as 3D printing laboratories, high-precision CNC testing beds, and licensed enterprise resource planning (ERP) software setups. Small manufacturing units can utilize these facilities via a subsidized pay-per-use model, removing high upfront capital barriers to advanced technology.

Factoring Green Transition into the ZED Certification

Link the existing Zero Defect Zero Effect (ZED) certification directly to concessional interest rates via a "Green Ribbon" credit window. Micro-enterprises achieving "Gold" or "Platinum" ZED compliance through sustainable practices (such as installing rooftop solar, implementing circular water recycling, or optimizing energy usage) should automatically qualify for 1.5% to 2% interest subvention on capital loans through public sector banks.

Modular Cadre of Corporate Mitras for Formalisation

Deploy a decentralized network of professional management support, functioning similarly to banking correspondents. Partner with professional bodies (such as ICAI and ICSI) to train local commerce graduates in tier-II and tier-III cities as certified "Corporate Mitras." Operating on a fixed, performance-linked government stipend, these professionals can directly handle digital GST filing, intellectual property filings, and corporate governance for micro-units, driving formalization from the ground up.

E-Commerce Export Fulfillment Hubs (EEFHs)

Set up specialized E-Commerce Export Zones near major international airports and multimodal logistics parks. These zones should offer single-window customs clearance, integrated warehousing, and automated digital tracking systems. By co-locating international courier desks with quality-testing laboratories, artisans and small electronics components manufacturers can dramatically reduce transit timelines and customs friction for overseas orders.

🏭 Conclusion: The transformation of India's MSME sector from a resilient shadow network into a scalable manufacturing powerhouse is essential for achieving strategic economic autonomy. By dismantling collateral-based credit bottlenecks, leveraging digital public infrastructure for cash-flow lending, and building shared technology hubs, India can resolve the "dwarfishness" trap and integrate small businesses into global value chains.
