The Constitutional Pivot: One Nation, One Election

How the 129th Amendment reimagines India's electoral rhythm, balancing fiscal efficiency against the basic structure doctrine

By Shoruto constitutional affairs team

The Constitution (129th Amendment) Bill, 2024, seeks to institutionalise One Nation, One Election (ONOE) by synchronising India's federal and national electoral cycles under Article 82A. While proponents highlight significant benefits like reduced fiscal deficits, optimised CAPF deployment, and administrative continuity, the roadmap faces severe friction. Ultimately, the system demands extensive constitutional adjustments to Articles 83 and 172, presenting deep structural challenges to India's basic structure doctrine and federal character.

What is One Nation, One Election?

Definition and Core Concept

One Nation, One Election (ONOE) is a proposal to synchronise the electoral calendar in India. The goal is to hold elections for the Lok Sabha (the lower house of India's Parliament), all State Legislative Assemblies, and local bodies (municipalities and panchayats) simultaneously within a single window. Currently, India is in a state of perpetual election mode—voters head to the polls somewhere in the country almost every few months as different state assemblies finish their five-year terms at different times.

Historical Evolution

Simultaneous elections to the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies were the standard practice in India from 1951 to 1967, spanning the first four general election cycles (1952, 1957, 1962 and 1967). This natural synchrony was disrupted during 1968–69 due to the premature dissolution of multiple State Legislative Assemblies. The cycle fractured completely when the 4th Lok Sabha (1967-1970) was dissolved ahead of schedule in 1970, leading to fresh national elections in 1971.

Commission and Committee Recommendations

  • 1983 Election Commission Report: The Election Commission of India (ECI) recommended a return to simultaneous elections to mitigate huge expenditures and administrative inconvenience.
  • 1999 Law Commission (170th Report): Chaired by Justice BP Jeevan Reddy, the Commission strongly urged implementing synchronised polls for the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies.
  • 2015 Parliamentary Standing Committee: It highlighted reduced costs and governance disruptions from the Model Code of Conduct (MCC), noting that the Anti-Defection Law and Article 356 safeguards would strengthen the federal balance during a phased rollout.
  • 2017 NITI Aayog Discussion Paper: The paper proposed a "phase-wise" synchronisation strategy explicitly designed to minimise constitutional disruption.
  • 2018 Law Commission Draft: It held that synchronisation requires legal amendments, proposing phased models, a constructive vote of no-confidence, and fixed-term adjustments to manage hung assemblies.
  • The Kovind Committee (2023–2024): The panel recommended a phased execution, first aligning the Lok Sabha and State Assemblies, followed by local body elections within 100 days, utilising fixed tenures and a common electoral roll.

Following the union government's official acceptance of these panel recommendations, the Constitution (129th Amendment) Bill, 2024, became the primary vehicle to "reset" India's electoral clock to the 1951–1967 era.

129th Constitutional Amendment Bill, 2024

Core Legal Definition: The Bill defines simultaneous elections as general elections held for the House of the People (Lok Sabha) and all State Legislative Assemblies together.

  • Synchronised Terms via Article 82A: Under the newly proposed Article 82A, the President will notify an "appointed date" (the first sitting of a new Lok Sabha), after which the terms of all newly elected State Assemblies will be adjusted or shortened to expire concurrently with that Lok Sabha's five-year term.
  • The "Unexpired Term" Rule (Articles 83 & 172): If the Lok Sabha or a State Assembly is dissolved prematurely, fresh elections will be conducted only for the "unexpired term" (the remaining period of the national five-year cycle) rather than starting a fresh five-year tenure, ensuring the synchronisation is not permanently broken.
  • ECI Deferral Discretion: The ECI holds the discretion to recommend deferring a specific state's election if localised exigencies arise, though that assembly's term will still terminate alongside the active Lok Sabha.
  • Strategic Exclusion of Local Bodies: To avoid the immediate constitutional mandate requiring ratification by at least half of the State Legislatures, the Bill excludes municipal and panchayat elections from this specific text at this stage.

Union Territories Laws (Amendment) Bill, 2024

  • Consequential Integration: Designed to ensure parity, this companion Bill extends the synchronisation rules to Union Territories with legislatures, specifically the National Capital Territory of Delhi, Puducherry, and Jammu and Kashmir.
  • Term Realignment: It legally binds these UT Assemblies to expire alongside the national Lok Sabha cycle, subjecting them to the same "unexpired term" structural parameters during premature dissolutions.

What are the Key Benefits of Holding One Nation, One Election in India?

Reduction in Public Expenditure and Fiscal Deficit

Holding isolated elections repeatedly forces both the Union and State exchequers to bear separate logistical costs. Under the existing rules, the Union government covers Lok Sabha election costs, State governments cover Assembly election costs, and the costs are split 50:50 only when they happen concurrently. According to the Centre for Media Studies (CMS), the 2024 General Election cost an estimated Rs 1.35 lakh crore, more than double the Rs 60,000 crore spent in 2019.

The Joint Parliamentary Committee (JPC) on 'One Nation, One Election' has said that simultaneous Lok Sabha, Assembly and local body elections can save nearly 7 lakh crore rupees by eliminating duplicate spending on security transits, procurement, and setup.

Elimination of MCC-Induced "Policy Paralysis"

The frequent enforcement of the ECI's MCC places an immediate freeze on developmental administration. During an MCC window (45-60 days), governments are legally barred from announcing new capital projects, launching welfare schemes, or executing crucial policy changes, which severely delays long-term capital investments. In states like Uttar Pradesh or Maharashtra, where local body elections, assembly polls, and national elections frequently drop in different years, administrative departments often spend 4 to 6 months out of a single year operating inside an MCC freeze. ONOE confines this policy restriction to a single, predictable window every five years.

Substantial Upliftment of National Macroeconomic Indicators

The High-Level Committee (HLC), chaired by former President Ram Nath Kovind, incorporated extensive macroeconomic modelling to assess how continuous election modes affect the broader economy. Frequent election cycles create a volatile environment of regulatory uncertainty that delays private capital investments. Findings presented by the JPC state that institutionalising ONOE could structurally boost India's GDP growth rate by up to 1.6% while simultaneously keeping inflation trends lower and highly predictable by stabilising public consumption spending shocks.

Maximising Administrative Continuity and Optimising Bureaucracy

Whenever an election is scheduled, the regional administrative machinery undergoes mass-scale structural disruption. District Magistrates, police chiefs, and down-the-line civil servants are routinely transferred or placed under direct ECI operational command. This structural diversion pulls the bureaucracy entirely away from routine public service delivery, grievance redressal, and basic day-to-day governance. Synchronised elections ensure that public officials can dedicate their energy to long-term structural execution for a stable, five-year block.

Reduction in the Diversion of Educational Infrastructure and Manpower

A major hidden casualty of India's continuous election cycle is the state public education system. Millions of public school teachers and administrative staff are regularly drawn into election duty as booth-level officers, polling officers, and counting supervisors. An Economic Advisory Council to the Prime Minister (EAC-PM) working paper highlighted that transitioning to an ONOE framework reduces the total physical deployment of polling personnel by nearly 28% (equivalent to 26 lakh personnel over a 5-year election cycle). This shifts the burden out of the system, effectively saving 1.04 crore personnel-days—the vast majority of which preserves standard instructional time for government school students.

Mitigation of Internal Security Overstretch and Forces Fatigue

Securing an election in India requires moving massive amounts of manpower. The Ministry of Home Affairs must constantly de-board, transport, and deploy hundreds of companies of Central Armed Police Forces (CAPF)—such as the CRPF, BSF, and CISF—alongside state police units to manage law and order. Continuous state elections subject these forces to endless transit cycles, causing severe fatigue and leaving structural internal security vulnerabilities elsewhere. Synchronising the dates allows for a single, highly coordinated, single-window strategic deployment plan nationwide.

Curtailing the Influence of Black Money and Campaign Finance Abuse

Frequent elections increase the continuous demand for political fundraising, which directly feeds into the systemic circulation of unaccounted cash or "black money" across the economy. Political parties and individual candidates are forced into an endless cycle of raising and spending undocumented funds to stay competitive. By compressing the entire electoral calendar into a single, unified block, the total volume of election-related cash transactions drops drastically, enabling the ECI to track, audit, and regulate campaign finance compliance much more effectively.

Reversal of Voter Fatigue and Boosting Overall Voter Turnout

With citizens being asked to cast ballots for municipal corporations, state assemblies, and the Lok Sabha at completely different intervals, widespread voter fatigue sets in. This fatigue routinely leads to low voter turnout, particularly across urban centres. By presenting a unified, high-stakes voting event where the citizen decides local, state, and national leadership in one visit, the civic importance of the exercise increases. Furthermore, a single, synchronised window makes it significantly easier for India's massive migrant labour population to travel back to their home constituencies once to cast all votes, significantly boosting the overall democratic turnout.

What are the Key Challenges of Holding One Nation, One Election in India?

Threat to the Basic Structure Doctrine

The Supreme Court in the Kesavananda Bharati case, 1973, held that parliamentary democracy and free and fair elections form part of the Constitution's basic structure—a threshold that even regular constitutional amendments cannot violate. Reaching a synchronised cycle demands extending or curtailing active state assembly terms. This directly conflicts with the foundational phrase "no longer" used in Articles 83 and 172, which was explicitly inserted by the Constituent Assembly to bar extensions beyond five years without a fresh mandate.

Distortion of Collective Responsibility

India's parliamentary tradition dictates that the executive holds office only as long as it commands legislative confidence. The proposed German model of a "constructive no-confidence motion" (which requires pairing a no-confidence vote with a simultaneous confidence vote for an alternative leader) could distort this principle and incentivise minority governments to cling to power without genuine popular support.

Flawed Economic and Cost Assumptions

Proponents cite economic models arguing that synchronised polls boost real GDP growth by 1.6%. However, India's actual economic history shows that the simultaneous election era (1952–1967) produced the low "Hindu rate of growth" (~3.5%), whereas the genuine high-growth era (8 to 9% between 2003 and 2011) flourished under asynchronous, staggered elections. Government expenditure on elections constitutes less than 0.1% of its budget. The real fiscal pathology is the unaccounted political cash—estimated at over Rs 1,00,000 crore for the 2024 Lok Sabha election alone—which no calendar readjustment can solve. ONOE merely concentrates this black money footprint rather than diminishing it.

Federal Deficit and Disadvantage to Regional Dynamics

In July 2026, a group of eminent citizens at the "One-Nation One-Election, Federalism and Citizenship" conclave in New Delhi rejected the synchronised polls initiative, warning it would fracture India's federal compact and curtail the constitutional autonomy of State Assemblies. Coinciding national and state polls induce a uniform voting pattern where voters tend to choose the same large national party at both tiers (Wave Effect). This structurally disadvantages regional parties, risks submerging local crises (such as agrarian distress in Vidarbha, coastal livelihoods in Kerala, or floods in Assam) under monolithic national campaigns.

Local governance centers on hyper-local issues like sanitation, primary health, and village infrastructure. Under ONOE, the massive political machinery of national general elections can easily drown out these grassroots concerns. This creates a vacuum in democratic decentralization (envisioned by the 73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1992), as voters may end up choosing local representatives based on national leadership preferences rather than local accountability.

Administrative and Governance Inefficiencies

While the current system imposes the MCC in a rolling fashion across different states, ONOE would concentrate the freeze nationally every 5 years. This simultaneously halts developmental and welfare announcements across every single state, amplifying governance disruption rather than eliminating it. In the case of mid-term dissolutions, fresh elections will be held only for the remaining "unexpired term" to keep the calendar in sync. This creates a high probability of legislatures surviving for exceptionally short tenures (even less than a year), leaving cabinets with insufficient time to govern effectively.

Monumental Financial Burden of Hardware Scaling

While proponents cite long-term savings, the upfront capital expenditure required to scale up voting infrastructure is unprecedented. For independent cycles, the same Electronic Voting Machines (EVMs) are systematically rotated across states. Under simultaneous polls, every single polling booth needs separate, dedicated machines for all tiers simultaneously. Official estimates submitted by the ECI to the Law Ministry indicate that a simultaneous rollout requires at least 46.75 lakh Ballot Units (BUs), 33.63 lakh Control Units (CUs), and 36.62 lakh VVPATs. The ECI estimated a recurring cost of roughly ₹10,000 crore every 15 years solely to replace and upgrade these machines, given their fixed lifespan.

Severe Overstretch of Central Armed Police Forces

India's current multi-phase election model relies heavily on the strategic, rolling deployment of Central Armed Police Forces (CAPF). Holding elections everywhere at once removes the ability to pool and rotate these specialised forces. Securing all polling stations simultaneously creates an acute deficit of trained central security personnel. Relying heavily on local home guards or unspecialized state police forces could severely compromise the security framework required for free and fair polling in sensitive zones.

What Steps are Needed to Implement the One Nation One Election System?

Build Organic Political and Inter-State Consensus

The Joint Parliamentary Committee (JPC), currently reviewing the legislation, must actively engage with regional and opposition parties to address fears of regional issues being overshadowed by national narratives. Securing voluntary resolution or state-level legislative consensus is critical, especially since aligning local bodies requires ratification by at least half of the State Legislatures.

Codify a "Truncated Mandate" Rule for Mid-Term Collapses

Legally codify the "Remainder Term" mechanism in the text of the 129th Amendment Bill, 2024. The law must state that if an assembly is dissolved prematurely, the mid-term poll is explicitly for a "bye-assembly"—meaning the newly elected MLAs only serve out the remaining months left in the original national 5-year window, immediately dissolving when the Lok Sabha does. Every State Assembly must formally amend its Rules of Procedure and Conduct of Business to mandate a "Constructive Vote of No Confidence" (the German model). Under this rule, a motion to topple a sitting Chief Minister is legally invalid and cannot be voted upon unless the opposition table simultaneously names a successor leader who already commands an absolute majority on the floor.

Synchronise Data Systems into a Unified Common Electoral Roll

The ECI should lead a massive data-integration drive to build a single, cross-platform Common Electoral Roll. By synchronising regional state databases with the national registry, the government can issue a single Electoral Photo Identity Card (EPIC) valid across all tiers of voting.

Formulate a Comprehensive CAPF Strategic Deployment Matrix

The Ministry of Home Affairs must design a dedicated National Security Deployment Matrix for ONOE. This should involve expanding the intake of Central Armed Police Forces (CAPF), optimising the inter-state transit grid via dedicated railway corridors, and training state police and home guards to handle non-sensitive booth management under unified command.

Institutionalise Strict Campaign Finance and Media Regulations

Alongside the structural election bills, Parliament should pass comprehensive electoral finance reforms. The ECI should implement strict, separate expenditure caps for Assembly and Lok Sabha candidates running in the same constituency, along with airtime allocation regulations on public and digital broadcasters to protect the voice of regional parties.

Issue Multi-Year Advance Capital Allocations to BEL and ECIL

The Ministry of Finance must issue a dedicated, non-lapsable budget line directly to Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL) and Electronics Corporation of India Limited (ECIL). This cannot be a standard year-of-election purchase order; it requires a 3-to-4 year advance manufacturing runway to scale up production to the required ~46 lakh Ballot Units and ~36 lakh VVPATs without supply-chain bottlenecks.

⚖️ Conclusion: The push for ONOE marks a systemic structural pivot toward administrative efficiency and fiscal optimization. However, its long-term viability hinges on navigating complex constitutional imperatives, maintaining the sanctity of the basic structure doctrine, and preserving the federal equilibrium. Realizing this balance requires a transparent legislative process built upon deep inter-state consensus.
