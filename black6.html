The Orange Economy: India's Creative Superpower

How digital penetration, youth-led content creation, and strategic interventions like ONCE and Creative Enterprise Zones are reshaping India's knowledge-driven future

By Shoruto creative economy team

India's Orange Economy is transitioning into a structural pillar of a knowledge-driven economy, driven by digital penetration and youth-led content creation. However, the sector faces severe bottlenecks, including rampant digital piracy, an original Intellectual Property (IP) deficit, and metropolitan clustering. To scale globally, India must implement strategic interventions like open-protocol networks and spatial Creative Enterprise Zones.

What is the Orange Economy and its Status in India?

About: Defining the Orange Economy

The Orange Economy, also known as the creative economy, refers to economic activities that rely on individual creativity, skill, and intellectual property (IP) as the primary inputs to generate value, jobs, and wealth. It is rapidly becoming a structural pillar of India's transition from a service-based economy to a knowledge-driven, "mindfacture" superpower.

Key Components

It encompasses a wide range of industries where human imagination is the "raw material," including:

  • Media & Entertainment: Film, television, radio, music, and digital content creation.
  • Design & Arts: Performing arts, visual arts, photography, architecture, fashion, and advertising.
  • Technology & New Media: Gaming, animation, and VFX.
  • Cultural Heritage: Handicrafts, traditional knowledge systems (e.g., Ayurveda), museums, and festivals.

Key Growth Drivers in India

  • Digital Penetration: India's 1 billion internet users and 700 million social media users have democratized content creation, allowing creators to reach global audiences directly via OTT and social platforms.
  • High-Growth Segments: Sectors like Animation & VFX and Live Events are witnessing rapid expansion. Digital media has overtaken traditional television as the dominant segment.
  • Youth & Creators: There are 2 to 2.5 million active digital creators in India whose work significantly influences consumer spending habits, particularly among Gen Z (born between 1997 and 2012).

Government Initiatives

  • Institutional Support: The Union Budget 2026-27 has prioritized the sector, proposing AVGC (Animation, Visual Effects, Gaming, and Comics) Content Creator Labs in 15,000 secondary schools and 500 colleges.
  • Policy Focus: The Ministry of Culture has established a dedicated Creative Economy vertical to provide market linkages and promote cultural exports.
  • Skill Development: Initiatives like the Indian Institute of Creative Technology, Mumbai aim to bridge the projected demand for 2 million AVGC professionals by 2030.
  • Heritage Branding: Efforts are underway to transform historic sites like Lothal, Sarnath, and Leh Palace into interactive, tech-driven heritage destinations.

What is the Significance of the Orange Economy For India?

High-Value Employment Generation

The creative economy is a massive employment engine that supports over 10 million direct and indirect livelihoods across the country, (approximately 8% of India's total workforce). Unlike traditional sectors, these jobs are creative occupations that pay significantly better—roughly 88% higher than non-creative roles. It provides high-productivity alternatives for India's youth demographic, moving beyond the low-wage trap of traditional manufacturing.

Decentralization of Economic Growth

Creative wealth is not limited to metropolitan hubs. Out of the top 10 creative districts in India, 6 are non-metros—Badgam (J&K), Panipat (Haryana), Imphal (Manipur), Sant Ravi Das Nagar (Uttar Pradesh), Thane (Maharashtra), and Tirupur (Tamil Nadu)—indicating the diversity and depth of creativity across India. This fosters inclusive regional development, allowing local crafts, textiles, and design-based industries to contribute to the national GVA without mass migration to urban centers.

Technological Leapfrogging via AVGC-XR

For India, the AVGC-XR (Animation, Visual Effects, Gaming, and Comics-Extended Reality) sector offers a strategic opportunity for technological leapfrogging by enabling rapid advancement in digital creativity, immersive technologies, and innovation ecosystems. Leveraging its young demographic, growing digital infrastructure, and creative talent pool, India can bypass traditional industrial stages and emerge as a global leader in next-generation entertainment and extended reality solutions. This convergence promises substantial economic growth, employment generation, and enhanced global competitiveness.

Strengthening Cultural Soft Power

The expansion of India's Orange Economy has emerged as a vital pillar of its economic diplomacy. According to data from the Export-Import Bank of India (Exim Bank), India's creative goods and services exports crossed $121 billion (with a $16 billion trade surplus in the creative goods industry) in 2019, driven heavily by segments like design (87.5%) and traditional crafts (9%).

Digitisation and Market Access

The Ministry of Information & Broadcasting (MIB) operationalised WaveX, India's premier national media-tech startup accelerator, implemented alongside the National Film Development Corporation (NFDC). Anchored by premier institutions like T-Hub Hyderabad and the Foundation for Innovation and Technology Transfer (FITT), IIT Delhi, WaveX is expanding a network of 10 institutional innovation hubs nationwide. This is further complemented by government-led distribution initiatives like Prasar Bharati's 'Creator's Corner' and the WAVES OTT platform, which bypass traditional gatekeepers to grant micro-influencers direct, secure algorithmic monetisation pathways and seamless access to international export markets.

Advancing Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

The formalisation of India's cultural and creative infrastructure acts as a structural multiplier for SDG 8 (Decent Work & Economic Growth) and SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities & Communities). This is backed by the Government of India's ₹1 Lakh Crore Urban Challenge Fund (2025-26 to 2030-31), which prioritises reform-linked, climate-resilient city infrastructure while preserving local craft clusters and historical zones. By transforming ideas into assets via digital networks and intellectual property, it generates substantial gross value-added (GVA) without requiring the massive ecological or carbon footprints typical of traditional industries.

What are the Key Concerns Facing the Orange Economy in India?

Rampant Digital Piracy and Revenue Leakage

The rapid expansion of high-speed internet has exacerbated digital piracy. It has shifted from physical bootlegs to sophisticated online streaming, Internet Protocol Television (IPTV), and peer-to-peer distribution networks. This severely cripples monetisation pathways for legitimate rights holders. In 2024, approximately 90 million users accessed pirated video content, resulting in $1.2 billion in revenue loss—equivalent to 10% of the legal video industry. If unaddressed, piracy is projected to grow to 158 million users by 2029, increasing cumulative losses to $2.4 billion and further constraining legitimate industry growth.

Deficit in Original IP Ownership

While India is a global hub for back-end technical execution in AVGC, it heavily functions as an outsourced service provider rather than a creator of original Intellectual Property. Indian studios typically earn basic service wages, while foreign studios capture the compounding, long-term financial "rents" via merchandise, sequels, and global licensing rights. The Animation, VFX and post-production (AVP) segment is largely work-for-hire and 85% to 90% of its revenues stem from providing services to IP owners and studios, while owned IP monetization is just 10% to 15%. Major franchises (like the Marvel Cinematic Universe) contract out substantial portions of their complex rotoscoping, VFX, and 3D modeling to clusters in Hyderabad, Bengaluru, and Pune. However, the multi-billion-dollar downstream licensing revenues stay entirely with western production houses, exposing the structural vulnerability of India's creative labor chain.

Severe Institutional Skilling Gaps and Supply Deficit

The creative industries are evolving into highly technology-driven domains requiring interdisciplinary mastery across coding, narrative design, real-time rendering, and spatial computing. But, the current formal education framework does not supply industry-ready professionals at the required pace. Meeting the target of 2 million AVGC professionals by 2030 requires an annual workforce increment approximately ten times the current supply from accredited programmes.

Algorithmic Monopolization and Platform Dependency

Independent creators and micro-entrepreneurs are increasingly dependent on a handful of global, centralized tech titans (e.g., Alphabet, Meta, ByteDance) for audience reach and monetization. India's 2 to 2.5 million active digital creators heavily influence over 30% of consumer choices, driving $350–400 billion in annual spending. Despite this immense economic power, they remain structurally vulnerable. A single change in a foreign platform's monetization policy or recommendation algorithm can instantly wipe out the financial viability of localized, vernacular digital media startups.

Asymmetric Value Capture

India's underappreciated Orange Economy layer resides in its traditional crafts, handlooms, and Geographical Indication (GI) products. However, due to a lack of formal financial literacy, direct digital market access, and structural protection, the financial premiums generated by these assets rarely reach the grassroots artisans. International luxury brands and upstream aggregators often capture the brand equity premium of GI tag products. The Prada–Kolhapuri chappal controversy underscored the vulnerability of traditional Indian cultural IP to global appropriation without fair trade reciprocity.

Severe Bureaucratic Friction for Live and Experiential Economies

The experiential segment of the creative economy—encompassing live music, performing arts, theatre, and cultural festivals—possesses substantial economic spillover potential for urban services and domestic tourism. However, executing physical events in India involves navigational hurdles across multiple overlapping jurisdictions. Organizing a major live cultural event requires securing 10 to 15 different No-Objection Certificates (NOCs) spanning local municipal bodies, fire safety, police, traffic, noise pollution enforcement, and copyright compliance agencies.

Geographical Concentration and Spatial Segregation of Creative Capital

Creative infrastructure, capital accumulation, and corporate studio networks are overwhelmingly concentrated in a few Tier-1 metropolitan clusters. This spatial polarization isolates the vast depository of rural and semi-urban talent from formal monetization channels, causing rural talent drain and unequal regional human development. Majority of the institutional venture capital funding and formal corporate revenue within the media, animation, and tech-design verticals is restricted to urban epicenters like Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Mumbai, Pune, Chennai, and Delhi-NCR.

What Strategic Interventions are Required to Scale India's Orange Economy?

Architectural Decentralization via ONCE

India can adapt its successful Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) model to establish open, equitable transactional pathways for independent creative practitioners. Implement Open Network for Creative Exchange (ONCE), a unified, open-protocol network designed on interoperable architecture similar to ONDC and UPI. ONCE will allow musicians, authors, visual designers, and folk artists to directly register, list, and license their works without platform intermediary friction. This sets up a "Sovereign Media Stack" that provides transparency in algorithmic discoverability and allows micro-creators to directly access domestic and international consumer bases.

Intellectual Property Fractionalization via Regulated Security Token Offerings (STOs)

Establish an institutional "IP Fractionalization Framework" under a dedicated regulatory sandbox managed by SEBI and the Ministry of Culture's Creative Economy vertical. This mechanism allows high-potential cinematic universes, gaming titles, or performing arts franchises to be divided into secure, digital asset fractions. Retail and institutional investors can acquire micro-equity in individual IPs, creating a highly liquid market for "Patient Creative Capital" powered by automated smart-contract royalty distributions.

Implementing Spatial "Creative Enterprise Zones" (CEZs) in Tier-2/3 Metros

Set up dedicated Creative Enterprise Zones (CEZs) in secondary growth corridors like Indore, Guwahati, Kochi, and Thiruvananthapuram, mirroring the successful Software Technology Parks of India (STPI) blueprint. These zones should feature state-subsidised, shared infrastructure—including High-Performance Computing (HPC) Cloud Rendering Farms, advanced spatial computing setups, and Dolby Atmos audio suites. This allows localised cultural expressions to be seamlessly upscaled into globally competitive digital products.

Constructing Proactive "Smart-Licensing Frameworks" for Generative AI

Codify a distinct "Right to Creative Persona and Style Protection" within standard intellectual property statutes. This framework introduces mandatory "Smart-Licensing Agreements" that require technological developers to pay automated micro-royalties whenever an artist's voice, distinct visual style, or registered literary text is integrated into foundational model training or commercial synthetic outputs, ensuring clear economic reciprocity.

Transitioning Fiscal Incentives toward "Anchor IP Production"

Restructure corporate tax frameworks to introduce an "IP-Accumulation Tax Holiday." While standard outsourced creative services operate under baseline fiscal rates, domestic creative entities that conceptualise, register, and retain Original Indian IP Rights can be granted complete exemptions on foreign royalty inflows for an initial five-year window. This intervention incentivises studios to build long-term, global merchandise and licensing pipelines.

Engineering Sovereign "Brand-Equity Pipelines" for Heritage Assets

Formulate structured, institutional trade channels that embed GI-tagged artisan clusters (like Pochampally Ikat weavers or Jaipur Blue Pottery) directly into international luxury retail networks and global design calendars. By integrating the India Cine Hub portal (facilitates film shootings in India for global and domestic filmmakers) with regional handicraft ecosystems, foreign film tourism and cultural experiences can be directly monetised to drive localised export demands for authentic Indian products.

Re-aligning Academic Training with "Creator-Entrepreneurship"

Position the Indian Institute of Creative Technologies (IICT) network to deliver specialised training in "Creative Venture Design." The upgraded academic curriculum should integrate advanced technical workflows (like Unreal Engine or Blender) with core commercial proficiencies—such as international syndication economics, global intellectual property litigation, copyright asset valuation, and algorithmic marketing preparing professionals for leadership roles in the global creative marketplace.

🎨 Conclusion: The Orange Economy marks India's shift from a service-oriented hub to a "mindfacture" superpower. By mitigating structural challenges like piracy and the IP ownership deficit through innovative models like ONCE and IP fractionalization, India can harness its youth demographic to solidify its standing as a global creative and cultural powerhouse.
